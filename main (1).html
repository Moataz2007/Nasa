marine phytoplankton

Half of all net primary production is from marine phytoplankton (unicellular plant-like organisms that perform photosynthesis). These phytoplankton form the base of the entire ocean ecosystem; they not only provide food for other animals in the sea and oxygen for us to breathe, but they also absorb carbon dioxide and enable carbon sequestration.

Here are several key reasons why phytoplankton form the base of the ocean ecosystem:
  1. **Primary Producers**:

    Phytoplankton are primary producers in the ocean, meaning they can convert light energy, primarily from the sun, into organic matter through the process of photosynthesis. This ability to create their own food makes them essential for initiating energy flow in marine food webs.

  2. **Energy Source**:

    Phytoplankton serve as the primary energy source for higher trophic levels in the ocean. They are consumed by zooplankton, which, in turn, become food for various marine organisms, including small fish, larger predators, and even marine mammals. Thus, phytoplankton provide the basis for the entire marine food chain.

  3. **Nutrient Recycling**:

    Phytoplankton play a crucial role in recycling nutrients in the ocean. They take up dissolved nutrients from the surrounding water, such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and iron, and incorporate these nutrients into their cells. When they are consumed and ultimately die, these nutrients are released back into the water, making them available for other organisms. This nutrient recycling is vital for sustaining life in the ocean.

  4. **Oxygen Production**:

    Through photosynthesis, phytoplankton release oxygen as a byproduct into the water. They provide significantly to the oxygen production in the world's oceans as it produces 50 percent of the oxygen in our atmosphere.

  5. **Global Carbon Cycle**:

    - Phytoplankton play a crucial role in the global carbon cycle by absorbing atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) during photosynthesis. - They are estimated to sequester roughly 40% of the world's annual CO2 emissions, helping to mitigate climate change.

  6. **Carbon Sequestration**:

    Phytoplankton are critical for the global carbon cycle. They absorb carbon dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere during photosynthesis. This process helps to mitigate climate change by removing CO2 from the atmosphere and storing it as organic matter in the ocean. The carbon is then transferred through the food web, buried in sediments, or transported to deeper ocean layers.

  7. **Base of Food Webs**:

    Phytoplankton support various trophic levels in the ocean, from zooplankton to apex predators like sharks and whales. Without a robust phytoplankton population, the entire oceanic food web would collapse, leading to the decline or extinction of many marine species.

  8. **Indicator of Ecosystem Health**:

    The abundance and health of phytoplankton can serve as indicators of overall ecosystem health and environmental conditions. Changes in phytoplankton populations can signal shifts in ocean temperatures, nutrient availability, and other factors that affect the entire ecosystem.

  9. **Global Significance**:

    Phytoplankton are not limited to a particular region but are found throughout the world's oceans, from surface waters to deep sea. Their global distribution and influence on climate make them a critical component of Earth's overall environmental balance.

Relation between global warming and oceans

  1. *Global warming's impact on the oceans: *

    Global warming has significant effects on the oceans and vice versa. According to reports from the UN Climate Change, the ocean has borne the brunt of human-caused global warming for a long time. As the Earth's largest carbon sink, the ocean absorbs excess heat and energy resulting from the increased emissions of greenhouse gases trapped in the planet's systems. Currently, the ocean absorbs approximately 90% of the heat generated by the rising emissions.

  2. *The consequences of global warming on the oceans: *

    • Rising seawater temperature: The increase in greenhouse gases has adversely affected the ocean's health, leading to a rise in seawater temperature and acidification. This has harmful repercussions for marine and terrestrial life, reducing the ocean's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide and protect life on Earth.
    • Sea-level rise: The rate of sea-level rise has accelerated due to ice loss in polar regions. Recent data from the World Meteorological Organization shows a record high in global mean sea-level in 2021, with an average annual rise of 4.5 millimeters between 2013 and 2021. This has intensified extreme events like storm surges, coastal hazards, and flooding.
    • Marine heatwaves: Marine heatwaves have become more frequent, prolonged, and intense, primarily due to human influence. These heatwaves have led to widespread coral bleaching and reef degradation. If ocean warming continues, all coral reefs worldwide could bleach by the end of the century.
    • Loss of marine biodiversity: Higher temperatures pose a significant risk to marine and coastal ecosystems, including coral reefs and mangroves. Species are migrating to cooler regions. Estimates suggest that over half of the world's marine species may be at risk of extinction by 2100, with substantial ecosystem degradation already at 1.1°C warming.

  3. *The oceans' impact on global warming: *

    The ocean plays a crucial role in our climate system. It generates 50% of the Earth's oxygen, absorbs 25% of carbon dioxide emissions, and captures 90% of excess heat from these emissions. It acts as both the planet's "lungs" and its largest "carbon sink," mitigating the effects of climate change. However, increasing greenhouse gas emissions have harmed the ocean's health, resulting in warming and acidification, which negatively affect both marine and terrestrial life. This has reduced the ocean's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide and protect life on Earth.

  4. *The role of ocean ecosystems like mangroves and coral reefs:*

    Habitats such as mangroves and coral reefs, along with their interconnected food webs, can capture carbon dioxide from the atmosphere at rates up to four times higher than terrestrial forests. Mangroves, in particular, are highly carbon-rich ecosystems. They also provide benefits such as supporting fisheries, improving water quality, and offering coastal protection against floods and storms. Coral reefs, covering a small fraction of the ocean, are ecologically and economically valuable, supporting a significant portion of marine biodiversity and benefiting billions of people.

  5. *The potential of ocean and wind energy: *

    The ocean represents an abundant source of renewable energy, including offshore wind and ocean energy. These sources, derived from natural processes like wind, water, and tides, do not emit carbon dioxide or other greenhouse gases that contribute to global warming. Harnessing wind energy throughout the ocean can significantly reduce thermal emissions and help combat climate change.

Renewable energy and oceans

Renewable ocean energy, often referred to as aqua power, represents a main source of sustainable electricity generation derived from the ceaseless movements and properties of Earth's oceans. Covering more than 71 percent of the planet's surface, oceans offer a vast and promising reservoir of clean energy. Ocean energy extraction primarily involves capturing the kinetic and potential energy within oceanic water movements, such as waves, tides, and currents, and converting it into electricity through specialized devices like underwater turbines and wave energy converters.

The unique significance of ocean energy lies in its abundance and sustainability. Unlike finite fossil fuels, ocean energy is perpetually generated by natural processes, making it a consistent and reliable source. This predictability allows for efficient energy planning and grid integration. Moreover, ocean energy production has a minimal environmental impact, with low greenhouse gas emissions and no need for resource extraction or transportation.

Coastal regions, which often have high energy demands, can particularly benefit from harnessing ocean energy, reducing the costs and losses associated with long-distance energy transportation. The diversity of ocean energy sources, including waves, tides, currents, and temperature gradients, offers flexibility in technology selection for different locations, enhancing scalability and adaptability.

Nonetheless, the widespread adoption of ocean energy faces challenges, including the need for technological advancements, infrastructure investment, environmental considerations, and supportive regulatory frameworks. As technology continues to evolve and costs decrease, renewable ocean energy is poised to significantly contribute to global clean energy objectives.

Deep-sea mining is gaining attention due to the growing demand for critical minerals like lithium, cobalt, and graphite, crucial for renewable energy technologies. However, it's a contentious practice with environmental and social concerns.

Mining and oceans

Deep-sea mining involves extracting valuable mineral deposits from the ocean floor, including polymetallic nodules, sulphides around hydrothermal vents, and metal-rich crusts on seamounts. Advances in technology have made it possible to mine these areas using underwater vehicles

Current status:While commercial deep-sea mining has not yet started, exploratory mining tests have taken place. The UN's International Seabed Authority (ISA) faces challenges in regulating the practice, with discussions ongoing until 2025.

Benefits: Proponents argue that deep-sea mining can meet the growing demand for critical minerals, especially as clean energy technologies expand. It could also potentially avoid some of the environmental and social issues associated with terrestrial mining.

How to preserve ocean and marine life

  1. Reduce resource consumption: Try to minimize water and electricity consumption in your home and use resources sustainably.
  2. Proper waste disposal: Sort your waste and recycle what you can, and dispose of hazardous waste safely.
  3. Embrace sustainability: Choose products made from recyclable and eco-friendly materials.
  4. Reduce plastic use: Avoid single-use plastics and opt for eco-friendly alternatives.
  5. Coastal conservation: Participate in environmental campaigns to clean beaches and coastal areas.
  6. Support environmental projects: Join environmental organizations or support projects aimed at protecting the oceans and marine life.
  7. Raise awareness: Share information about the importance of preserving the oceans with others and engage in awareness campaigns.
  8. Respect environmental laws and regulations: Adhere to local and international laws aimed at protecting the oceans.
  9. Clean investment portfolio: Ensure that your investments do not support companies harming marine ecosystems.
  10. Contribute to research: If you are a scientist or researcher, contribute to environmental research and marine studies.
